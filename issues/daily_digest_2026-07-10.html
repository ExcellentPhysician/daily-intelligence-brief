CHS Up 500% Since 2016 — Haloperidol Now Confirmed Over Ondansetron

TrendingClinical Update

Emergency departments are seeing a dramatic and ongoing surge in cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome (CHS) — up over 500% since 2016, driven by increasingly potent cannabis products. Medscape EM reports that ED physicians now have a growing array of treatments available.

The key clinical update: the randomized Haloperidol vs. Ondansetron trial confirmed haloperidol's superiority — better nausea control, reduced pain scores, and shorter ED stays. Ondansetron is largely ineffective for true CHS.

Action item: if your ED still defaults to Zofran for cyclic vomiting in cannabis users, the evidence now clearly supports Haldol 5mg IV as first line for confirmed CHS.

Annals of Emergency Medicine · Must Read
Medetomidine in the Illicit Drug Supply: A New Crisis Naloxone Cannot Reverse

Must ReadToxicology

Lynch, Pizon & Yealy published in Annals of Emergency Medicine on medetomidine — a veterinary alpha-2 agonist now contaminating the fentanyl supply, especially in Philadelphia but spreading nationally. The CDC has an active situation summary.

Critical point: naloxone does NOT reverse medetomidine. Patients present with atypical withdrawal — anxiety, tremor, flushing, diaphoresis within 4–6 hours of last use, progressing to refractory nausea/vomiting and severe tachycardia/hypertension. Standard opioid withdrawal protocols fail entirely.

If your ED sees "naloxone-resistant" OUD patients with atypical withdrawal, think medetomidine. Alpha-2 agonist withdrawal — not opioid withdrawal — is the mechanism.

July 9–10, 2026 · Wearable Cardiac AI
On-Body AI Patch Detects Lethal Arrhythmias with 99.6% Accuracy — No Cloud, No Delay

CardiologyWearable AIJuly 9, 2026

Researchers at the University of Chicago have developed a flexible computing patch that detects life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias with 99.6% accuracy — and processes all data on the body itself, requiring no cloud connectivity and introducing no latency. The neural network runs entirely on a chip embedded in a flexible substrate that conforms to the chest wall.

What it solves: Ambulatory cardiac monitoring has historically required either hospitalization for telemetry or cumbersome external Holter monitors with delayed cloud-processed results. This patch eliminates both constraints — real-time classification, continuous monitoring, no connectivity required.

Why it matters for EM: This directly erodes one of the most common admission reasons from the ED — "chest pain with concerning rhythm, needs monitored bed." As these devices reach clinical availability, expect pressure to shift observation-level cardiac admits to outpatient ambulatory monitoring protocols. Academic EDs at systems like MetroHealth will feel this transition within 2–3 years.

Also from this week: CMS established a new Office of Health Technology Products (OHTP) — the first dedicated CMS infrastructure to oversee AI, interoperability, and digital health tools. This matters for future reimbursement pathways for AI diagnostics.

Ohio Senate Passes SB 423 — Healthcare Independent Contractor Classification Bill

OhioWorkforce

The Ohio Senate approved SB 423 on July 7 — a bill defining when healthcare workers placed through online shift platforms qualify as independent contractors versus employees. It specifies 15 criteria, including formal agreements, ability to reject shifts without penalty, and prohibition of noncompete clauses.

The bill now moves to the Ohio House, though it may not receive a vote until after the November election. The American Staffing Association has raised concerns about patient care impact.

Relevant for any 1099 locums or per-diem shift work you do through platforms — IC classification protections would be meaningfully strengthened if enacted.

Ohio's FY2026-2027 operating budget (HB 96) remains under Senate deliberation with healthcare funding amendments still pending.

H.R.2062 — Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act Advances

Active BillTax Deductible

H.R.2062, introduced by Rep. Mike Kelly (R-PA), Greg Murphy, MD (R-NC), and Chris Smith (R-NJ), would amend the Internal Revenue Code to classify HCSM membership payments — including sharing amounts and administrative fees — as deductible medical expenses.

If enacted, this represents thousands in annual tax savings for the estimated 1.5+ million Americans enrolled in plans like Samaritan Ministries, Liberty HealthShare, or Sedera.

This bill has bipartisan physician sponsorship (Murphy, MD is a surgeon). Its path is narrow but real in the current Congress.

IRS Guidance · 2026
IRS Proposes Letting HRAs Reimburse HCSM & DPC Shares Tax-Free

HRAIRS Rule

The IRS issued a proposed rule expanding HRA reimbursement eligibility to include monthly HCSM share payments, DPC fees, and certain public coverage payments — classifying them as qualified medical expenses for tax purposes.

Critically, the guidance does not reclassify sharing ministries as insurance — it simply recognizes these payments as eligible care expenses, a meaningful practical win even without full legislative tax parity.

For Tabbut Real Estate LLC: if you offer employees an HRA, this proposed rule opens a path to offer HCSM-based coverage as a legitimate tax-advantaged benefit structure.
