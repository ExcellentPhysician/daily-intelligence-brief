Top Highlight

Medetomidine Now Spreading Nationally in Illicit Fentanyl Supply

A new article in Annals of Emergency Medicine by Lynch et al. details the alarming spread of medetomidine — a potent veterinary alpha-2 adrenergic agonist — now detected in fentanyl samples across Philadelphia, Pittsburgh, Chicago, and New York (25.1% of samples by 2025). Unlike xylazine, medetomidine produces prolonged sedation, bradycardia, and biphasic blood pressure changes that persist after naloxone. The withdrawal syndrome is severe and often requires ICU-level care. The CDC issued a Health Advisory urging clinicians to expand toxicology screening and recognize that standard opioid overdose protocols are insufficient. This is heading to Cleveland — be prepared.
Practical note: naloxone will not reverse medetomidine sedation. Alpha-2 agonist reversal with atipamezole may be needed but is not FDA-approved for humans. Update your ED protocol now.

Health Sharing Ministries

HCSM · Legislation · Tax Treatment
Federal Legislation · 119th Congress

Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act Introduced (H.R. 2062)

U.S. Reps. Mike Kelly (R-PA), Greg Murphy, MD (R-NC), and Chris Smith (R-NJ) introduced H.R. 2062, the Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act, in the 119th Congress. The bill would classify regular HCSM membership payments as tax-deductible medical expenses under the Internal Revenue Code — the same treatment currently given to traditional insurance premiums. Currently, HCSM members pay with after-tax dollars, creating a significant tax disadvantage compared to employer-sponsored insurance. The bill has bipartisan support signals given health sharing's growing appeal as an alternative to ACA plans. 107 groups are currently certified by HHS as qualifying HCSMs. State-level fiscal analysis shows minimal revenue impact (~$477K/year decrease per state), making this legislatively achievable.

IRS Guidance Update

IRS Proposed Rule: HRAs May Reimburse HCSM Fees Tax-Free

Separately from H.R. 2062, the IRS issued a proposed rule in 2026 that would expand Health Reimbursement Arrangements (HRAs) to cover HCSM membership fees tax-free — a significant practical win even without the Tax Parity Act passing. The rule classifies HCSM fees alongside direct primary care payments and certain public coverage payments as qualified medical expenses eligible for HRA reimbursement. Importantly, the IRS makes clear this does not redefine HCSMs as insurance — it simply treats sharing fees as a legitimate medical expense. For physicians or entrepreneurs offering health benefits to employees (e.g., through your LLC), this potentially opens a path to offering HCSM-based health benefits via an Individual Coverage HRA (ICHRA). See guidance at Take Command Health and HSA for America.
