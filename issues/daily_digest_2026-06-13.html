Top Journal Article · Annals of EM
Medetomidine in the Illicit Drug Supply: A New EM Crisis

ToxicologyAnnals · 2026

Lynch, Pizon & Yealy's article "Emergence of Medetomidine in the Illicit Drug Supply: Implications for Emergency Care and Withdrawal Management" in Annals of Emergency Medicine is essential reading for urban EM physicians. Medetomidine — a veterinary alpha-2 agonist first detected in Maryland illicit samples in 2022 — has now spread to multiple states, appearing in fentanyl supplies in Philadelphia, Pittsburgh, and Chicago.

Key clinical points:

  • Diagnosis of acute toxicity and withdrawal is clinical — no FDA-approved immunoassay exists
  • No reversal agent approved for humans (unlike naloxone for opioids)
  • Withdrawal syndrome differs from opioid withdrawal — anticipate hypertension, agitation, tachycardia
  • Alpha-2 agonist toxidrome: bradycardia, hypotension, sedation, miosis

With MetroHealth's inner-city catchment, this belongs in your clinical awareness today.

Reconciliation 2.0 Signed: Medicaid Work Requirements Now Law

Signed Into LawJun 9, 2026

The House passed S. 2 — Republicans' second major reconciliation package — on June 9, 2026, with the Senate having approved it the week prior. The president signed it into law the same day. Building on the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 4, 2025), this package extends Medicaid work requirements and further restricts the ability of states to use provider taxes to fund Medicaid expansions.

The combined Medicaid impact across both bills: the CBPP projects hundreds of billions in additional state Medicaid funding cuts. The AMA has called this legislation an estimated 11.8 million people losing coverage across both bills combined. For an inner-city academic ED like MetroHealth, uninsured volume shifts are the real operational consequence.

The National Law Review federal health update (June 12) notes states have limited runway to implement new verification systems before the 2028 compliance deadline.

Ohio Policy · June 10, 2026
Ohio Lawmakers Pull Back on Medicaid Home Healthcare Restrictions

Ohio LegislatureJun 10, 2026

After more than 200 Ohioans testified against a proposed bill that would have banned family members from being paid by Medicaid to care for disabled relatives at home, Ohio lawmakers significantly scaled back the legislation, per the Ohio Capital Journal. The revised bill now focuses narrowly on giving the state attorney general expanded authority to investigate fraud allegations — the controversy that triggered the legislation in the first place.

Governor DeWine, who defended the program publicly for weeks, cited the economics: home health services cost at least four times less than nursing facility care. Ohio had also suspended 49 home health providers over fraud concerns and proposed a GPS-tracking EVV requirement for providers.

"No one wants to go to an institution; no one wants to go to a nursing home. They want to stay in their own home." — Gov. DeWine

The Ohio Senate is also working through hundreds of amendments to the FY 2026–27 state operating budget (HB 96), with OSMA actively lobbying on Medicaid physician reimbursement rates.

NEJM Perspective · May 2025
Primary Care as Free-Market Commodity: NEJM Raises the Alarm

WarningNEJM

Song and Zhu's "Primary Care — From Common Good to Free-Market Commodity" (NEJM 392(20):1977–1979) frames the DPC/concierge exodus as a social equity problem: as physicians take "exit ramps toward the free market" for higher income and restored joy, the cost falls on the patients left behind in traditional primary care.

The piece acknowledges the genuine appeal — DPC physicians escape prior auth, documentation burden, and the time pressure of volume-driven medicine. But it argues the externalities are real: shorter access windows for Medicaid and safety-net patients, and increased strain on the PCPs who remain.

Worth reading as the counterweight to the DPC enthusiasm: the market offers physicians an exit, but not every patient gets to follow.

Market Structure · 2026
Doctors Band Together: IPAs as the Free-Market Middle Ground

IPAMarket Power

KFF Health News and NPR document the growing movement of primary care physicians forming Independent Physician Associations (IPAs) to negotiate collectively with payers — a middle path between full hospital employment and the solo DPC model. IPAs allow physicians to retain independence while gaining the negotiating leverage of scale.

As of 2026, there are nearly 3,000 DPC practices operating in the U.S. The HSA DPC bill — which would allow HSA funds to cover DPC membership fees — has been introduced but not enacted. IPAs represent the structural answer for physicians who want autonomy without burning their insurance relationships entirely.

The IPA model is particularly relevant to emergency medicine groups negotiating with hospital systems and insurer contracts.

Federal Legislation · 2025–2026
Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act: Status Update

Tax PolicyFederal Bill

Reps. Mike Kelly (R-PA), Greg Murphy (R-NC), and Chris Smith (R-NJ) introduced the Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act, which would classify regular HCSM membership payments as eligible medical care expenses — allowing them to be deducted just like health insurance premiums.

Currently, HCSM payments are not tax-deductible even for members who use them as their primary healthcare cost-sharing mechanism. The bill would close this asymmetry. The HSA for America 2026 update notes that new legislation does allow sharing ministry payments to be reimbursed tax-free alongside certain MEC plans — an incremental win while the larger bill awaits a floor vote.

HHS has certified 107 HCSM groups meeting the federal definition. Tax parity remains the number-one policy ask from the sector.
