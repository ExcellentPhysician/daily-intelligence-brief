Top Highlight · Medscape, Jun 12 2026
Should Epinephrine Leave Shockable Arrest?

In a pointed Medscape debate, Drs. Robert Glatter and Peter Antevy argue epinephrine may actively worsen outcomes in shockable cardiac arrest, and that early defibrillation, double sequential external defibrillation (DSED), and high-performance CPR should take priority instead. Antevy: electricity and good CPR matter — "epinephrine is just getting in the way and needs to go away."

They lean on the 2022 NEJM DOSE-VF trial showing DSED roughly doubled neuro-intact survival in refractory VF, and flag VA-ECMO as the next step for true refractory cases reaching the ED.

Top Journal Article · Annals of EM, Vol 87 Iss 6
Mistriage Quietly Delays Care

A retrospective cohort of ED encounters across 21 EDs (2016–2020), just published in Annals of Emergency Medicine by Sax, Warton, Mark, et al., found both under- and overtriage produce measurable delays in care — undertriaged high-acuity patients waited roughly 8 extra minutes compared to correctly triaged peers, with downstream effects on ICU admission and short-term mortality.

Worth a department-level look at triage calibration, especially relevant given growing interest in AI-assisted triage decision support.

Healthcare IT News · Jun 25, 2026 (no stronger item in past 24h)
HIMSS Forum: AI Sustainability Needs Smaller, Governed Models

At the HIMSS AI in Healthcare Forum in Boston, leaders from UMass Memorial Health and Stanford Healthcare argued that hospital AI has to move past pilot-stage automation toward governed, edge-deployed "smaller models" if it's going to scale sustainably across clinical decision support and precision medicine use cases. The session also flagged a widening "digital divide" — well-resourced systems can build and iterate on AI, while smaller community and safety-net hospitals are increasingly priced out.

Why it matters for an academic ED: as MetroHealth and peer systems evaluate AI triage and documentation tools, governance infrastructure — not just model capability — is becoming the gating factor for adoption. Separately, the ZS Impact Institute's 2026 Future of Health Report found 52% of patients now use AI to research symptoms before ever calling a clinician — a pattern with direct implications for ED triage conversations.

Federal · Final Rule, May 28 2026
No Surprises Act: IDR Fees Cut 85%

HHS, Labor, Treasury, and OPM jointly finalized the Federal IDR Operations final rule, cutting the administrative fee for disputing out-of-network claims from $115 to $15 per party — an 85%+ reduction — and streamlining the arbitration process under the No Surprises Act.

ACEP, joined by the American College of Radiology and American Society of Anesthesiologists, applauded the move; ACEP President Dr. L. Anthony Cirillo called it "meaningful progress" toward a dispute process that's "predictable, efficient, and grounded in transparency." Directly relevant to any EM group's out-of-network billing exposure.

Doctors Band Together as IPAs to Build Leverage

With Medicaid cuts and value-based reimbursement squeezing margins, independent primary care physicians are increasingly forming Independent Physician Associations (IPAs) to negotiate collectively with payors rather than selling out to hospital systems or PE roll-ups.
