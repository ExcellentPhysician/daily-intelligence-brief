AI-Read ECG Catches Heart Failure an ED Missed

Note: no single item broke in the strict past-24 hours; this is the strongest item from the past several days, dated explicitly per freshness policy.

Pathway Labs announced FDA clearance on June 23 for EchoNext, the first multicondition AI tool to read a standard 12-lead ECG and flag six categories of hidden structural heart disease — right- and left-sided heart failure, valve disease, severe hypertrophy/infiltrative cardiomyopathy, and pulmonary hypertension — conditions a normal ECG read typically misses. Pathway Labs simultaneously announced a partnership with OpenEvidence, the clinical-AI platform already used by more than half of U.S. clinicians.

The clinical stakes were made concrete in a Nature Medicine case report published June 22: a patient presented to a NewYork-Presbyterian/Columbia emergency department with four days of worsening dyspnea after wildfire-smoke exposure, was read as an asthma flare with normal labs, and was discharged — exactly the kind of borderline ED disposition every attending makes weekly. EchoNext's retrospective ECG read flagged the structural disease that labs and a normal chest exam missed, leading to a diagnosis and ultimately the world's first AI-detected heart transplant.

Why it matters for an academic EM physician: this is a credible signal that ECG-based AI screening could become a real second check on the "looks stable, normal labs, send home" disposition call — worth watching as FDA-cleared tools move from cardiology into ED workflow.

Federal
No Surprises Act Fix Cuts IDR Fees 85%

House Ways and Means Committee Chairman Jason Smith (R-MO) praised the new CMS final rule (the same IDR rule covered in the EM section) for fixing what he called years of "Biden-era" dysfunction in surprise-billing dispute resolution, which previously took an average of 150 days — and as long as 268 — to resolve.

Concrete changes: provider IDR filing fees drop from $115 to $15 per claim (an 85% cut), the batching threshold for claims doubles from 25 to 50 items, and IDR entities now face a 5-business-day eligibility-determination deadline.

Ohio
Senate Passes Gig-Nursing Platform Bill

The Ohio Senate unanimously passed Senate Bill 423, sponsored by Sen. Susan Manchester (R-Waynesfield), which writes legal definitions for healthcare-worker staffing platforms — the apps that connect nurses and techs to facility shifts — and clarifies when those workers are not employees of the platform or facility for purposes of Ohio's pay and overtime law.

Directly relevant to staffing models at NE Ohio systems like MetroHealth as hospitals lean further into app-based shift coverage. The 112-page bill was referred to the Ohio House on June 23; the General Assembly is not expected back in session until after the Nov. 3 election, so action stalls until then.

DPC / HSA Policy
The New HSA-DPC Rule Has a Compliance Trap

As of January 1, 2026, patients in qualifying high-deductible plans can use HSA funds to pay DPC membership fees — but KevinMD reports the fix shipped as tax policy, not access policy. IRS Notice 2026-05 caps eligible monthly fees at $150 for individuals / $300 for families; one practice pricing membership at $160/month caused patients who paid with HSA dollars to lose HSA contribution eligibility for the entire year.

Context: there are now nearly 3,000 DPC practices nationally across Pure, Hybrid, and on-site models, with typical fees of $20–$50/month for kids and $50–$100/month for adults — most of which sit safely under the new cap, but not all.

Physician Autonomy
Doctors Band Together for Market Power

KFF Health News documents the continuing growth of Independent Physician Associations (IPAs) as primary care doctors push back against Medicaid cuts and value-based reimbursement squeeze. The IPA Association of America says it now represents over 300,000 physicians.

The catch, per the AAFP: not every IPA preserves autonomy — some are quietly owned by hospital systems or private equity. The Academy's advice is to vet for "integrity" and real physician decision-making power before joining.

Federal
Tax Parity Bill Still Pending in Congress

H.R. 2062, the Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act, remains active in the current 119th Congress. The bill would let HCSM members deduct membership and sharing payments as a qualified medical expense — closing a gap where the roughly 1.3 million Americans who share over $1 billion in medical costs annually through ministries get no tax treatment comparable to insurance premiums.

The Alliance of Health Care Sharing Ministries continues to lobby for passage; no floor vote has been scheduled.

State — Oklahoma
A State Deduction Could Be the Model

Oklahoma lawmakers are advancing a bill that would create a state income-tax deduction for contributions to health care sharing ministries, starting with tax year 2027 — per KGOU. If it passes, Oklahoma would become a template other red states — including Ohio — could copy while the federal Tax Parity Act sits in committee.
