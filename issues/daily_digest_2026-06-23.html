Clinical Debate
Does Epinephrine Make Shockable Arrest Worse?

In a Medscape Emergency Medicine discussion published June 12, Drs. Robert Glatter and Peter Antevy argue epinephrine may actually worsen outcomes in shockable cardiac arrest, and that early defibrillation, dual sequential defibrillation (DSED), and high-performance CPR should take priority over the drug.

A live debate worth tracking as resuscitation protocols keep drifting away from reflexive ACLS dosing.

Medetomidine Is Showing Up in the Illicit Drug Supply

A new review in Annals of Emergency Medicine by Michael J. Lynch and Donald M. Yealy flags medetomidine — a veterinary sedative — emerging as a contaminant in the illicit drug supply, with withdrawal management implications that don't track standard opioid-withdrawal protocols.

Worth a read before the next unusual-presentation overdose lands in triage.

Patient Care Tech · Dated Coverage, See Note
An AI Stethoscope Is Catching the Heart Failure Pregnancy Care Misses

Mayo Clinic researchers found an AI-enabled digital stethoscope — capturing ECG and heart-sound data at a routine prenatal visit — identified twice as many cases of peripartum cardiomyopathy as usual obstetric care, in a 1,195-patient trial. Cardiomyopathy is a leading cause of maternal mortality, and its symptoms (shortness of breath, swelling, rapid heartbeat) overlap heavily with normal pregnancy complaints — exactly the borderline presentation that also walks into the ED.

Freshness note: the most recent substantive coverage of this trial is from earlier this year, not the past 24–48 hours. No comparably strong patient-care-technology story broke in today's search window, so this is flagged here rather than presented as breaking news, per the freshness standard for this section.

DPC · HSA Policy
HSAs Can Now Pay Direct Primary Care Fees

Under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, DPC membership fees officially became HSA-qualified medical expenses as of January 1, 2026 — capped at $150/month for individuals, $300/month for families — without disqualifying members from HSA contributions, provided they keep a qualifying HDHP. Arguably the single biggest regulatory tailwind for the DPC model since its founding.

National Law Review Source

Trend · Concierge
Concierge and DPC Practices Grew 83% in Five Years

New Health Affairs research from Johns Hopkins, Harvard, and OHSU found concierge/DPC practice sites grew 83.1% between 2018 and 2023, with clinicians in those practices up 78.4%. The catch: corporate ownership of these practices jumped from 9% to a third over the same period — raising the question of whether the model stays physician-led or becomes the next platform play.

$1,000 Recommendation
Put It Into the Backdoor Roth, Not More BTC

With real estate, 1099 income, and direct BTC/MSTR exposure already on the balance sheet, the case for fresh capital into more crypto is weaker than the case for tax-advantaged diversification. At physician income levels, direct Roth IRA contributions are phased out, but the backdoor Roth — a traditional IRA contribution immediately converted to Roth — remains fully legal for 2026, with a $7,500 limit.

A $1,000 contribution into a low-cost broad index fund inside that wrapper buys diversification away from concentrated crypto/real-estate risk, with permanent tax-free growth.
