Top Journal · NEJM · Jun 25, 2026
ARISE FLUIDS: Vasopressors First vs. Fluids First in ED Septic Shock

Practice-ChangingNEJM 2026

The ARISE FLUIDS trial — the Australasian Resuscitation in Sepsis Evaluation: Fluid or Vasopressors in Emergency Department Sepsis — published its primary results in NEJM on June 25, 2026. The 1,000-patient multicenter RCT (Australia, New Zealand, Ireland) randomized patients 1:1 to restricted fluids + early vasopressors vs. larger initial fluid volume + delayed vasopressors.

Primary endpoint: days alive and out of hospital at day 90. Secondary endpoints include 90-day all-cause mortality, ventilator-free days, and vasopressor-free days. This is the definitive ED-based RCT on a question you manage every shift — fluid responsiveness vs. early norepinephrine in septic shock resuscitation.

Why it matters to you: MetroHealth runs high-volume septic shock. If ARISE FLUIDS confirms early vasopressor superiority, expect ACEP and SCCM guideline updates within 12 months.

Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act Reintroduced in Congress

LegislationHCSM

Reps. Mike Kelly (R-PA), Greg Murphy, M.D. (R-NC), and Chris Smith (R-NJ) introduced the Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act, which would allow Americans who are HCSM members to deduct their monthly sharing contributions as eligible medical care expenses on their federal taxes — the same treatment currently afforded to traditional insurance premiums.

Currently, approximately one million Americans use health sharing programs as their primary healthcare coverage vehicle. This legislation would remove a significant cost disadvantage relative to ACA-compliant plans and employer-sponsored insurance, potentially accelerating HCSM growth substantially.

If this passes, expect HCSM enrollment to surge — especially among self-employed physicians, DPC practices, and faith-based small businesses. A strong topic for your health sharing content series.

IRS Guidance · 2026
IRS Proposes Rule Expanding HRA Reimbursement for Health Sharing Contributions

IRSHRA + HCSM

The IRS has issued a proposed rule that would expand HRA eligibility to allow employers to reimburse employees tax-free for their monthly health sharing ministry contributions — classifying HCSM "shares" as qualified medical expenses. This doesn't reclassify sharing ministries as insurance, but it gives them functional parity for tax purposes in employer-sponsored HRA structures.

As Wolters Kluwer notes, a key question remains unanswered: whether HCSM members can still deduct the individual health insurance deduction under IRC §213(d). Legislators pushing the Tax Parity Act above are trying to close exactly this gap. Combined with the OBBBA's DPC/HSA alignment, the regulatory environment for alternative healthcare coverage models is materially improving in 2026.
