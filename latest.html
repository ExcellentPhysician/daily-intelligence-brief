Journal Article — Annals of Emergency Medicine · 2026
Medetomidine in the Illicit Drug Supply: A New Toxidrome That Outlasts Naloxone
Lynch, Pizon & Yealy (University of Pittsburgh) describe the rapid spread of medetomidine — a potent veterinary alpha-2 adrenergic agonist — as a fentanyl adulterant in the U.S. illicit drug supply. First detected in Maryland in 2022, it has now reached Philadelphia, Pittsburgh, and Chicago. The core clinical challenge: medetomidine produces prolonged sedation, bradycardia, and biphasic blood pressure that persist after naloxone administration. Standard opioid overdose management is insufficient. Withdrawal presents as severe autonomic instability resembling clonidine withdrawal. The CDC has issued a national Health Alert Network (HAN) notice. Full text available via Annals of Emergency Medicine. Know this drug before you meet it.

Residency & Training
EM Residency Applications Switch from ERAS to Residency CAS Starting in 2026
Emergency medicine is departing the Electronic Residency Application Service (ERAS) this cycle, moving to the Residency Centralized Application Service (Residency CAS). Cited drivers include reduced applicant cost, enhanced "holistic" review tools, improved data access for programs, and streamlined technology infrastructure. For Associate Program Directors and faculty reviewers, this means new reviewer dashboards, revised evaluation formats, and a learning curve for the 2026-2027 application cycle. Advising medical students and residents on navigating the new system is now an immediate practical task. ACEP Now and the Society for Academic Emergency Medicine are publishing transition resources.

Policy Analysis — KevinMD · June 2026
The DPC-HSA Rule Fixed the Tax Code, Not the Access Problem
Since January 1, 2026, qualifying HDHP enrollees can use Health Savings Account (HSA) funds to pay DPC membership fees — a long-sought policy change. But a pointed analysis on KevinMD argues the rule's real significance is architectural, not affordability: it embeds DPC formally within the tax-advantaged healthcare financing system and accelerates the employer plan integration documented in Hint's 2026 Trends Report. The author notes the implementation complexity — the rule is a tax provision, not an access mandate — and flags that navigating HSA eligibility with DPC fees still requires careful plan design. For physician-entrepreneurs building DPC businesses or advising patients, understanding this distinction between tax parity and meaningful affordability is essential framing.

Federal Legislation
HCSM Tax Parity Act Reintroduced; Ohio Among States Weighing State-Level Deduction
Reps. Mike Kelly, Greg Murphy, and Chris Smith have reintroduced the Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act, which would grant HCSM members the same federal income tax deductibility for their monthly sharing contributions that traditional insurance policyholders and self-employed individuals already enjoy. If enacted, this would meaningfully reduce the after-tax cost of health sharing for 1099 physicians and solo practitioners. Meanwhile, Oklahoma advanced a state-level income tax deduction for HCSM members, with Ohio explicitly named as a state under active consideration for similar legislation. Nationally, approximately one million Americans belong to certified health sharing ministries, with 107 groups certified by HHS.

IRS Guidance
IRS Proposes HRAs May Now Reimburse Health Sharing Contributions Tax-Free
A new IRS proposed rule — analyzed by Take Command Health — would expand Health Reimbursement Arrangement rules to allow employers to reimburse employee HCSM monthly "shares" on a tax-free basis. The same rule proposes qualifying direct primary care fees as eligible HRA medical expenses, creating a potential DPC + HCSM hybrid that could be employer-funded, tax-advantaged, and free from traditional insurance overhead. For self-employed physicians and small practice owners, this opens a possible structure: employer HRA funding DPC membership plus HCSM contributions, achieving near-comprehensive coverage at substantially lower cost than group insurance. The rule is still in proposed form — comment period is open — but the direction of IRS travel is clear. HSA for America has a 2026 update on the full tax treatment landscape.
