Clinical Debate · Jun 12, 2026
Does Epinephrine Belong in Shockable Arrest?

Writing in Medscape, Drs. Robert Glatter and Peter Antevy argue epinephrine may actively worsen outcomes in shockable cardiac arrest, making the case that early defibrillation, double sequential external defibrillation (DSED), and high-performance CPR should take priority over the reflexive epi push.

The piece lands alongside in-hospital registry data showing more frequent epinephrine dosing — including the standard every-3-to-5-minute interval — correlates with worse outcomes in both shockable and non-shockable rhythms.

Annals of Emergency Medicine
Mistriage's Hidden Time Cost

A new Annals of Emergency Medicine study by Sax and Reed found mistriage carries a real time penalty: undertriaged high-acuity patients waited 8 extra minutes for care, while overtriaged low-acuity patients saw a 3-minute care delay and a 42-minute longer total ED length of stay versus correctly triaged peers.

Useful ammunition next time someone argues ESI assignment doesn't matter operationally.

Mount Sinai's Bet on Managing AI Sprawl, Not Just Buying More of It

As health systems pile AI tools into imaging, generative documentation, and emerging agent-based workflows, Mount Sinai Health System partnered with AI-management platform Signal 1 to centralize intake, approval, monitoring, and ROI tracking across its entire AI portfolio.

"Our priority is ensuring we can monitor performance, safety, and impact at scale without slowing innovation," said chief digital transformation officer Robbie Freeman. It's a tell on where 2026 healthcare AI is heading: the bottleneck has shifted from finding tools to governing them — echoing Innovaccer CEO Abhinav Shashank's blunter framing the same week: healthcare AI "has a production problem, not an innovation problem." For an academic ED piloting its own AI workflows, the lesson holds — governance infrastructure has to scale alongside adoption.

Federal · Courts · Jun 12, 2026
Judge Guts Key Pieces of the ACA "Marketplace Integrity" Rule

Maryland District Judge Brendan Hurson ruled that CMS exceeded its authority in several provisions of the 2025 "Marketplace Integrity and Affordability" rule, vacating eight provisions — including one letting insurers revoke guaranteed coverage for consumers with past-due premiums, and another eliminating the 60-day income-correction window.

Hurson left the rule's premium-adjustment methodology changes intact, finding those better grounded in statute — a partial win for the cities, patient-advocacy groups, and small-business organizations that sued.

Ohio Moves to Regulate Gig-Staffing Apps in Healthcare

The Ohio Senate unanimously passed Senate Bill 423, creating the state's first legal framework for app-based platforms connecting independent nurses and other healthcare workers with short-staffed facilities — and spelling out when those workers qualify as 1099 contractors rather than employees.

Sponsor Sen. Susan Manchester (R-Waynesfield) introduced the 112-page bill after the Ohio Nurses Association reported 91% of healthcare workers say their workplace isn't always adequately staffed. SB 423 now heads to the Ohio House — worth tracking given Cuyahoga County's own staffing pressure at systems like MetroHealth.

Has Primary Care Become a Free-Market Commodity?

A NEJM Perspective argues primary care has drifted from being treated as a common good to a commodity bought and sold on the open market — part of why direct primary care and concierge models keep gaining members.

Membership-style DPC practices, often starting around $99/month for unlimited visits and basic labs, are filling the gap as the number of Americans who can't find a primary care doctor has grown 20% in the past decade, per KFF Health News.

Congress · H.R. 2062
A Tax Deduction for Health Sharing Members, Finally?

Reps. Mike Kelly (R-PA), Greg Murphy, M.D. (R-NC), and Chris Smith (R-NJ) introduced the Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act (H.R. 2062), which would let the roughly 1.3 million Americans in health care sharing ministries deduct their membership payments as a medical expense.

That would put HCSM dues on the same tax footing as traditional insurance premiums for the first time. A Senate companion, S.653, is also pending.

HRAs May Soon Reimburse Sharing Ministry Dues Tax-Free

The IRS has proposed expanding which expenses an HRA can reimburse tax-free to include health care sharing ministry payments. The guidance doesn't reclassify HCSMs as insurance — it simply treats ministry dues as an eligible care expense.

Practically: a member paired with a compliant MEC plan and an employer HRA could see ministry payments reimbursed alongside it.
