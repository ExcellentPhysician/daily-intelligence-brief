Journal Spotlight · Annals of EM
Medetomidine in the Illicit Drug Supply: What Every EM Physician Needs to Know
ToxicologyAnnals 2026

Study: "Emergence of Medetomidine in the Illicit Drug Supply: Implications for Emergency Care and Withdrawal Management" — Lynch, Pizon & Yealy, University of Pittsburgh Department of Emergency Medicine & Division of Medical Toxicology. Published Jan 22, 2026.

The clinical problem: Medetomidine ("tranq," "rhino tranq," "mede") is an alpha-2 agonist increasingly adulterating the illicit fentanyl supply. The dangerous twist: after naloxone reverses the opioid component, medetomidine sedation persists — patients appear to "re-narcotize" but naloxone re-dosing does nothing. Withdrawal is also poorly understood and potentially cardiac in nature.

Key takeaway: Patients presenting with fentanyl OD who remain sedated after adequate naloxone should raise immediate suspicion for medetomidine co-exposure. Standard opioid withdrawal protocols do not apply.

EM Staffing Watch
PeaceHealth–EEP Standoff Reaches Its Conclusion: What It Means for Independent EM Groups

As of July 1, 2026, PeaceHealth officially ended its 35-year contract with the independent Eugene Emergency Physicians (EEP) group and transitioned to ApolloMD, an Atlanta-based staffing company. The case has drawn national attention because it mirrors a recurring pattern: large health systems terminating long-standing relationships with independent physician groups in favor of corporate staffing models.

The implications extend well beyond Oregon. ACEP Now editors flagged the case as deserving "close attention from emergency medicine leaders nationwide," noting it illustrates the vulnerability of even deeply established independent groups to hospital system consolidation pressure. For anyone thinking about the free market medicine space in EM, this is the canonical counterexample — and a reason DPC/direct EM models may hold appeal.

On-Body AI Patch Detects Life-Threatening Arrhythmias with 99.6% Accuracy — No Cloud Required
WearablesAI DiagnosticsJuly 2026

What it is: Researchers at the University of Chicago have developed a flexible computing patch that detects life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias with 99.6% accuracy — processing all inference directly on the device, not in the cloud. The patch sits on the chest, runs a custom edge AI model, and delivers results in milliseconds.

Why it matters for EM: Cloud-dependent cardiac monitoring must transmit data, process it remotely, and return a signal — adding seconds that can be clinically decisive in a V-fib or SVT event. This device eliminates that latency entirely. In an ED context, it has implications for both post-arrest monitoring and rapid triage in undifferentiated chest pain patients.

Who's behind it: University of Chicago bioengineering lab, reported in MarketScale's July 2026 digital health signal roundup. The same report also notes a newly established CMS AI Office — a federal infrastructure signal that the government is moving past pilot programs toward institutional AI adoption in healthcare billing and operations.

Ohio Policy · July 2026
Ohio Senate Passes SB 423: Clarifying Independent Contractor Status for Healthcare Workers
Ohio SenateSB 423

The Ohio Senate passed SB 423, legislation that spells out specifically when a healthcare worker can be classified as an independent contractor — particularly for workers placed through online staffing platforms. The bill lists 15 requirements, including formal agreement terms, the ability to reject shifts without penalty, and a ban on noncompete clauses for platform-placed workers.

The bill now moves to the Ohio House for consideration. If passed, it would formalize legal protections that matter enormously to the growing number of EM physicians doing locums and gig-based emergency coverage — and has implications for the PeaceHealth/EEP-style conflicts playing out in EM staffing nationally.

Also in Columbus: the Ohio General Assembly continues deliberations on the FY 2026-2027 state operating budget (HB 96), with hundreds of health-related amendments pending. Ohio Medicaid funding levels remain contested.

IRS Proposed Rule · 2026
IRS Moves to Let HRAs Reimburse Health Sharing Ministry "Shares" Tax-Free
IRS RuleTax Treatment

The IRS issued a proposed rule that would expand the ability of Health Reimbursement Arrangements (HRAs) to reimburse employees' monthly HCSM shares tax-free. The proposed guidance classifies HCSM shares — along with fees for direct primary care — as qualified medical expenses eligible for HRA reimbursement.

What changes: This doesn't redefine HCSMs as insurance, but it significantly improves their after-tax economics for employees receiving HRA benefits from employers. For small business owners and self-employed physicians like 1099 workers, it opens a new avenue to reduce the after-tax cost of health sharing coverage.

The rule is in proposed form and open for public comment. Take Command Health has a good lay summary of the practical implications.

Legislation · Congress
Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act Reintroduced in House
House BillTax Deductibility

U.S. Representatives Mike Kelly (R-PA), Greg Murphy, MD (R-NC), and Chris Smith (R-NJ) reintroduced the Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act, which would allow Americans enrolled in HCSMs to deduct monthly sharing contributions as medical expenses — the same deductibility currently available to traditional insurance premiums.

The bill has a simple premise: if you're covering your family's healthcare costs through a faith-based sharing ministry, the IRS should treat that the same as an insurance premium for deductibility purposes. Approximately 1 million Americans use HCSMs. The legislation aligns with the broader trend in the IRS proposed rule above and reflects growing congressional interest in creating tax parity for alternative coverage models.

For any physician recommending health sharing to patients or considering it personally, these two developments together represent the best tax-treatment news the HCSM space has seen in years.
