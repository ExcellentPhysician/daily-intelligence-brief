Top Journal Article
Medetomidine Hits the Illicit Drug Supply — What EM Needs to Know

ToxicologyLynch & Yealy's review in Annals of Emergency Medicine covers the emergence of medetomidine — a veterinary alpha-2 agonist far more potent than xylazine — as an opioid adulterant. Withdrawal is severe (hypertensive emergency, tachycardia, vomiting) and naloxone won't touch the sedation.

The authors extrapolate from dexmedetomidine literature to suggest management approaches. CDC field notes already document ED detections in Missouri, Colorado, and Pennsylvania — assume it's in Cleveland's supply too.

Featured Technology · Past Week
On-Body Computing Patch Detects Arrhythmias With 99.6% Accuracy — No Cloud Required

WearablesEdge AIA flexible cardiac patch developed at the University of Chicago detects life-threatening arrhythmias with 99.6% accuracy — and processes all data on the body itself rather than offloading to a cloud, per DistilINFO's July 6 roundup. On-device processing sidesteps the latency, battery, and privacy problems that plague cloud-tethered wearables.

Why it matters to you: ED discharge of low-risk palpitations and syncope patients increasingly leans on ambulatory monitoring; clinical-grade edge AI patches could shift that follow-up from 30-day event monitors to real-time detection. It also lands the same week CMS stood up its new Office of Health Technology Products to govern exactly this category of AI-enabled devices.

Proposed IRS Rule: HRAs Could Reimburse HCSM Shares Tax-Free

TaxAn IRS proposed rule would let HRAs reimburse health care sharing ministry monthly shares tax-free, treating HCSM shares — along with DPC fees — as qualified medical expenses. If finalized, this is the biggest tax-treatment shift for the ~1 million HCSM members in years.

Wolters Kluwer's analysis flags the unresolved question: how far the qualified-expense definition stretches. Track final rule timing — it directly affects how you'd structure family health costs.
