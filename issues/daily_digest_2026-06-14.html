ACEP Applauds IDR Final Rule — Stronger Enforcement Now the Key

ACEP is publicly applauding the Independent Dispute Resolution (IDR) Operations Final Rule released May 28, calling it a meaningful win for emergency physician reimbursement under the No Surprises Act. The rule now requires insurers to submit all information needed for clean claim processing before the IDR clock starts — cutting off the bad-faith delay tactics that have plagued the process. ACEP's president stressed that "the rule's overall success will depend on strong, consistent enforcement and meaningful consequences for noncompliance." For EPs, this directly affects out-of-network payment disputes that have caused significant revenue leakage since 2022. Watch ACEP Now for enforcement development this fall.

Journal Article

Medetomidine in the Illicit Drug Supply: Critical ED Reading

A clinically urgent piece in Annals of Emergency Medicine covers medetomidine — a veterinary alpha-2 agonist now contaminating the illicit opioid supply in multiple U.S. cities. Unlike xylazine, medetomidine causes profound bradycardia and hypotension alongside deep sedation, creating a distinct toxidrome that may only partially respond to naloxone. The article addresses: ED recognition criteria, hemodynamic management, the distinct withdrawal presentation, and the complexity of poly-drug coingestants. This is material reading before your next MetroHealth shift — the inner-city academic ED is exactly the setting where this is already showing up. Pre-read and brief your residents.

FY27 LHHS Bill Advances — Congress Blocks HHS Restructuring Proposals

The House Appropriations LHHS Subcommittee released its FY2027 LHHS appropriations bill June 4, with subcommittee markup June 5 and full committee markup June 9. The bill explicitly rejects the Administration's proposals to restructure and consolidate public health programs — signaling bipartisan resistance to large-scale reorganization of HHS agencies including CDC and HRSA. Separately, an interim final rule clarified Medicaid verification requirements with tighter state compliance deadlines through 2028. Ohio Lawmakers Reverse Course on Medicaid Home Healthcare Restrictions

Ohio lawmakers backed down from proposed restrictions on Medicaid home healthcare services after significant pushback from patient advocates and providers, per the Ohio Capital Journal (June 10). Governor DeWine publicly stated that Ohioans "want to stay in their own home" rather than enter institutional or nursing home care. The reversal followed a May visit to Ohio by federal Medicaid chief Dr. Mehmet Oz, who had raised concerns about potential misuse of home healthcare billing. In parallel, Ohio and federal authorities announced Medicaid fraud indictments in June. The episode reflects the tension between Ohio's 2026–2027 budget provision that would end Medicaid expansion if the FMAP drops below 90% — and the political reality of cutting services to vulnerable populations near MetroHealth's patient base.

Federal Legislation

Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act Introduced in Congress

Reps. Mike Kelly (R-PA), Greg Murphy, M.D. (R-NC), and Chris Smith (R-NJ) introduced the Health Care Sharing Ministry Tax Parity Act, which would classify HCSM membership payments as eligible medical care expenses for federal tax deduction purposes — equivalent treatment to traditional health insurance premiums. This is the central legislative ask of the health sharing movement and the most significant federal bill on HCSM in years. If folded into fall 2026 budget reconciliation, it could be transformative for HCSM enrollment economics. No Senate companion bill announced yet. As someone evaluating or recommending HCSMs in your faith community at excellentphysician.com, this bill is worth tracking weekly.

State Action · Oklahoma

Oklahoma Advances HCSM State Tax Deduction — With an Audit Requirement

Oklahoma lawmakers advanced a bill creating a state income tax deduction for HCSM members — one of the more meaningful state-level HCSM expansions in recent years. Significantly, the legislation includes a mandatory annual financial audit of participating ministries, a consumer protection provision long demanded by HCSM critics. If enacted, the audit requirement may actually strengthen movement credibility with skeptical physicians and policymakers. On the federal tax question: HSA for America notes that HCSM payments remain nondeductible at the federal level in 2026 absent passage of the Tax Parity Act — making state-level wins like Oklahoma's interim stepping stones worth celebrating.
